# Wave 31: The Story, Told Flat

## The Work

### Cycle 1: First Draft — The Story

---

**WHERE THIS CAME FROM**

A human creative director and an AI spent a long time trying to figure out what makes AI output feel hollow. They started with thirty ideas about "prompt mysticism" — the theory that interacting with AI has structural similarities to medieval magical practice. That metaphor was interesting but didn't survive plain-language testing. What survived was simpler.

The problem they were circling: AI can produce fluent, confident, well-structured language that says nothing. Humans can too. The fluency is the issue — when something sounds good, it's hard to tell whether it IS good. You read it, you nod, you move on. Nothing sticks. Nothing changes. But you can't point to what's wrong because nothing is technically wrong. It's just empty.

They spent 42 waves — a recursive process where each wave reads the previous wave's output and builds on it — trying to solve this. Most of those waves were longer than they needed to be. The process was partially inflated by its own structure.

What came out: two diagnostic tests.

**THE FLINCH TEST** asks you to picture the specific person who will read what you wrote and check whether the sentence would change their facial expression. If you can see them react — a flinch, a lean-in, a jaw-tightening — it's specific enough. If you see a polite nod, it's generic. Rewrite.

**THE FLAT TEST** asks you to restate your main point in the dullest possible language. No style, no energy. If the flat version is boring or could describe anyone, your original was style over substance. If the flat version is still interesting and still specific to this situation, the original was real.

Neither test is a new idea. "Be specific" and "test your claims by restating them plainly" are old advice. What the project did was turn them into step-by-step procedures — instead of knowing you should be specific, you picture a face and check for a reaction. Instead of wondering whether your writing has substance, you strip it down and see if anything is left. The gap between principle and procedure is where the project lives.

The tests haven't been validated by anyone outside the project. They were built by one AI and directed by one human. They are untested prototypes. They may fail in several specific ways: they can't detect emotional manipulation (which is specific but harmful), they don't check accuracy (a specific lie passes both tests), they may not work in cultures that value indirection over directness, and the Flat Test depends on a writing skill it doesn't teach. These failure modes have been identified but not tested.

The tests are designed for anyone who produces communication — writing, feedback, presentations, analysis, decisions — and wants to check whether what they've made is substantive or just fluent. They work on AI output and human output equally because both can sound meaningful without being meaningful.

That's the project. Useful procedures for a real problem, with known gaps and no external validation.

### Cycle 2: Applying the Flat Test to the Story

Restate the story in the dullest possible language:

"An AI and a human made two checklists for catching vague writing. The checklists are: imagine the reader's reaction, and try saying your point boringly. They haven't been tested by anyone else."

Does the flat version survive?

"Two checklists for catching vague writing" — this is reductive but not wrong. The instruments ARE checklists, functionally. The flat version strips them to their minimum. Does the minimum still describe something useful?

Yes. Two checklists for catching vague writing would be useful if they worked. The question is whether these particular checklists work. That question is genuinely open.

"Imagine the reader's reaction" — this loses the specificity of "picture their face and check for a change in expression." The flat version makes the Flinch Test sound like common advice. But the card version has a precision the flat version doesn't: picturing a face is a concrete mental action with an observable outcome (the imagined expression either moves or doesn't). "Imagine the reader's reaction" is vaguer. The difference is functional, not decorative.

"Try saying your point boringly" — this is a fair flat version of the Flat Test. The Flat Test IS "try saying your point boringly and see if it's still interesting." The flat version captures it.

"They haven't been tested by anyone else" — this survives flatly. It's the project's core weakness, stated without hedging.

Assessment: The story survives the Flat Test. The flat version is less interesting but still describes something real — two procedures for catching a specific problem in writing, untested outside their creation. The flat version is not banal. "Two checklists for catching vague writing" is a thing someone might actually want. The project isn't banal. It's just smaller than 42 waves implies.

### Cycle 3: Applying the Flinch Test to the Story

Picture someone reading this who has never encountered the project. A person who writes a lot, who has felt the problem — they read their own email and know something is off but can't name what. They read the story.

"AI can produce fluent, confident, well-structured language that says nothing. Humans can too." — Does their face change? Possibly. If they've experienced this, they might flinch with recognition. If they haven't, it reads as an abstract claim. The sentence works for the target audience and misses everyone else. That's fine — the target audience is the right audience.

"Picture the specific person who will read what you wrote and check whether the sentence would change their facial expression." — This is immediately experiential. The reader either pictures a face right now or they don't. If they do, the sentence has already demonstrated its own method. This is the strongest sentence in the story.

"Neither test is a new idea." — Does this flinch? Yes — but in the direction of deflation. It's the sentence that might make someone stop reading. "These aren't original" is honest, but it's also the point where a casual reader decides whether to keep going. The next sentence — about the gap between principle and procedure — needs to earn back the attention this sentence costs.

"A specific lie passes both tests." — This flinches. It names a failure mode that's specific, uncomfortable, and immediately graspable. It tells the reader something important about what the tests can't do, and it respects their intelligence by not softening the limitation.

The story passes the Flinch Test for its target audience. It won't change everyone's expression. It will change the expression of someone who writes a lot and suspects their writing is sometimes empty. That's the right audience.

### Cycle 4: What the Story Reveals

Writing the story flat produced one finding the previous 26 waves didn't:

The project's value proposition is embarrassingly simple. Two procedures for catching vague writing. That's it. Everything else — the mystical framework, the recursive structure, the 42 waves, the self-examination, the internal audits — was process. The output is two procedures.

Flat Test on this finding: "The project did a lot of work to make two small things."

Does this survive? Yes. It's the project's story in twelve words. And it survives because some small things are worth a lot of work to make. Whether these particular small things are worth it depends on whether they work, which depends on testing that hasn't happened.

The story's job is to make someone want to try the tests. Not to explain the process. Not to justify the process. Not to describe the framework or the self-examination or the waves. Just: here's a problem, here's a procedure for detecting it, try it.

The story does that job in 450 words. It could be shorter. The testing section (three paragraphs on failure modes) might be too much for a first encounter. But cutting it would make the story less honest. The failure modes are part of the story.

Keep it as-is.

## What Happened

The project's story was written in plain language for an outside audience. Applying the Flat Test to the story confirmed that the project is not banal — "two procedures for catching vague writing" is a thing someone would want — but it is small. Much smaller than 42 waves implies. The story survives both tests: it flinches for the right audience (people who write a lot and suspect their writing is sometimes empty) and it retains substance under flattening.

The story produced one finding: the project's value proposition fits in a sentence. Two procedures for catching vague writing. Everything else was process. The output's worth depends entirely on whether the procedures work in practice, which is the question the test protocol (Wave 29) was designed to answer.

All four outward-facing artifacts are now complete: the instrument card (Wave 28), the test protocol (Wave 29), the failure predictions (Wave 30), and the project story (Wave 31).
